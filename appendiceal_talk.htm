Appendiceal neoplasms

Low-grade mucinous Neoplasms (LAMN)

  • Low-grade epithelial neoplasm of the appendix
  • Mucinous epithelium with “pushing” growth pattern
    • Not infiltrative pattern with desmoplasia
  • 0.5% of all GI neoplams
  • 2.1% of appendectomy specimens

Low-grade Mucinous Neoplasms (LAMN) Typical Pathology

  • Distinct “pushing border” invasion (NOT infiltrative with desmoplasia)
  • Atrophy of the appendiceal wall
  • Loss of lymphoid tissue
  • Effacement of the muscularis mucosa
  • Common mucin pools that can extend into serosa and beyond

Low-grade Mucinous Neoplasms (LAMN) Typical Pathology

  • Microsatellite stable (MSS)
  • BRAF V600E negative
  • KRAS mutations frequently present (approximately 50% of cases)
  • GNAS mutations may be present

In the ovary, this would be a cystadenoma

Not invasive - equivalent of a polyp

If rutured, can cause PMP = pseudomyxoma peritonei

Mucinous adenocarcinoma

In the ovary - mucinous carcinoma

High-grade appendiceal mucinous neoplasm (HAMN)

Appendiceal Epithelial Neoplasms WHO Classification (2019)

  • Serrated lesions and polyps
  • Mucinous neoplasms (LAMN, HAMN)
  • Adenocarcinoma
  • Goblet cell adenocarcinoma (GCA)
  • Neuroendocrine neoplasms

Low-grade mucinous Neoplasms (LAMN) Staging AJCC 9e - Tumor

  • T1 and T2 are NOT applicable to LAMN
  • pTis (LAMN): Confined to muscularis propria (including acellular mucin or mucinous epithelium)
  • pT3: Acellular mucin or mucinous epithelium extends into subserosa
  • pT4a: Acellular mucin or mucinous epithelium involves serosa of appendix or mesoappendix
  • pT4b: Direct invasion of adjacent organs/structures

Low-grade mucinous Neoplasms (LAMN) Staging AJCC 9e - Metastasis

  • pM1a: Intraperitoneal acellular mucin WITHOUT identifiable tumor cells
  • pM1b: Intraperitoneal metastasis with peritoneal mucinous deposits CONTAINING tumor cells
  • pM1c: Metastasis to sites other than peritoneum

Pseudomyxoma Peritonei (PMP)

Clinlical syndrome

  • Progressive accumulation of gelatinous mucinous ascites
  • Diffuse peritoneal deposits

Typically results from rupture of a mucinous appendiceal neoplasm

Clinical Features

Common presentations:

Gradually increasing abdominal girth (most common)

Ovarian mass in women (often right-sided)

New-onset hernia in men (sac filled with mucin)

Appendicitis (from ruptured appendiceal mucocele)

Characteristic distribution:

“Omental cake” (thickened, infiltrated omentum)

Right and left hemidiaphragm undersurfaces

Right subhepatic space, splenic hilum

Abdominal gutters, pelvis, cul-de-sac

Spares mobile portions of small bowel (critical surgical feature)

Prognosis by Peritoneal Disease Grade

Acellular mucin (pM1a): Low risk of progression, especially if confined to right lower quadrant

Low-grade cellular mucin: Intermediate prognosis

High-grade cellular mucin with signet ring cells: Much worse prognosis

Clinical Presentation Diagnosis

Presenting Symptoms

Acute appendicitis (most common)

Abdominal pain or mass

Incidental finding on imaging

Mucocele on imaging

Diagnostic Workup

Initial evaluation:

CT abdomen/pelvis with oral and IV contrast

Complete blood count, comprehensive metabolic panel

Tumor markers: CEA, CA 19-9, CA-125

Colonoscopy (to rule out synchronous colorectal malignancies)

For suspected metastatic disease:

CT chest/abdomen/pelvis

Consider diagnostic laparoscopy by experienced surgeon

Assess Peritoneal Cancer Index (PCI) score

Obtain biopsies and/or appendectomy if feasible

Note: FDG-PET/CT is NOT routinely indicated

Surgical Management

Localized Disease (Appendix Only)

Standard approach:

Negative margin appendectomy is sufficient for LAMN confined to appendix

Ileocecectomy may be considered if appendectomy not feasible

Right hemicolectomy is NOT routinely recommended for LAMN

Critical surgical principle: Avoid unnecessary right hemicolectomy. Studies show approximately 20% of patients undergo pre-referral right hemicolectomy without oncologic benefit—no nodal metastases are typically found in LAMN.

Margin considerations:

If positive margins: Consider cecal cuff resection

Perforation is a risk factor for peritoneal recurrence → warrants closer surveillance

Peritoneal Disease

Surgical principles:

Refer to experienced peritoneal surface malignancy surgeon

Focus on DIAGNOSIS during initial exploration, not extensive resection

Staging laparoscopy helpful to estimate PCI and extent of surgery required

Cytoreductive Surgery (CRS) + Intraperitoneal Chemotherapy (IPCT):

Indications:

Peritoneal-only disease with cellular mucin

Multi-quadrant acellular mucin

Select cases with localized acellular mucin

Goals:

Optimal cytoreduction (CC score 0 or 1)

NOT debulking—complete cytoreduction is the goal

Organ preservation when possible

IPCT agents:

Mitomycin C (most commonly used, preferred)

Oxaliplatin (alternative, less preferred if patient receiving IV oxaliplatin)

Candidacy determined by:

Patient’s overall health

Disease biology and extent

Tumor resectability

Multidisciplinary evaluation

Special Considerations

Acellular mucin localized to periappendix:

Observation if appendectomy already performed, OR

Localized CRS ± IPCT

Complete CRS not possible:

Observation

Clinical trial

Consider palliative CRS ± IPCT if symptomatic

Consider systemic therapy (with early discontinuation if no response)

Repeat CRS/IPCT:

May be considered for patients with prior CRS

Particularly for low-grade peritoneal tumors with limited spread

Time since prior surgery important for high-grade disease

Prognosis Outcomes

Disease Confined to Appendix

Excellent prognosis:

No recurrences reported in multiple studies with median follow-up of 5-6 years

Essentially benign behavior when completely resected

Some experts advocate for term “mucinous adenoma” for these cases

Peritoneal Disease

Overall outcomes:

5-year survival: 79-86%

10-year survival: 45-46%

Recurrence-free survival after CRS/HIPEC: 94% at 5 years

Prognostic factors:

Most important: Presence and cellularity of peritoneal deposits

Completeness of cytoreduction (CC score)

Bulk of peritoneal disease

High-grade cytology

Architectural complexity

Risk of Progression

Overall recurrence risk: 4-6% at 5-10 years for localized disease

Higher risk features:

Extra-appendiceal cellular mucin

Mucin beyond right lower quadrant

M1b staging

Tumor size 2 cm with acellular mucin in RLQ (paradoxically higher risk)

Perforation

Median time to recurrence: 23-24 months (most within first 3 years)

Surveillance Recommendations (NCCN Guidelines)

Resected Localized Disease

For completely resected LAMN confined to appendix WITHOUT high-risk features:

Surveillance may NOT be needed For other localized disease:

History physical: Every 6-12 months × 2 years, then annually up to 10 years

Tumor markers (CEA, CA 19-9, CA-125): Every 6-12 months × 2 years, then annually up to 10 years

CT or MRI abdomen/pelvis: Every 6-12 months × 2 years, then every 12-24 months up to 10 years

Colonoscopy if not done preoperatively

FDG-PET/CT NOT indicated

Resected Metastatic Disease

History physical: Every 6 months × 2 years, then annually up to 10 years

Tumor markers: Every 6-12 months × 2 years, then annually up to 10 years

CT or MRI chest/abdomen/pelvis: Every 6 months × 2 years, then every 12 months up to 10 years

Colonoscopy if not done preoperatively

Indications for further workup: Rising tumor markers

Unexplained abdominal symptoms

Documented recurrence

Radiographic progression

Key Controversies Pitfalls

Common Errors in Management

Unnecessary right hemicolectomy: LAMN rarely has nodal metastases; appendectomy with negative margins is sufficient

Routine diagnostic laparoscopy: Low yield in era of high-quality cross-sectional imaging

Aggressive surgery at initial presentation: Focus should be on diagnosis, not extensive resection

Inadequate pathologic evaluation: Entire appendix should be submitted for histologic evaluation in dilated specimens

Diagnostic Challenges

Extensive epithelial denudation in markedly dilated LAMNs makes diagnosis difficult

Distinction from ruptured diverticula (look for wall atrophy, loss of lymphoid tissue)

Determining cellularity of peritoneal mucin (requires thorough sampling)

Discordant cases: Low-grade appendiceal tumor with high-grade peritoneal disease

Management Controversies

Role of HAMN management (some advocate right colectomy)

Optimal surveillance intensity and duration

Role of systemic chemotherapy (limited data, generally not responsive)

Timing and candidacy for repeat CRS/HIPEC

Take-Home Points

LAMN is a distinct entity with “pushing” invasion, NOT infiltrative growth like adenocarcinoma

Appendectomy with negative margins is sufficient for localized disease—avoid unnecessary right hemicolectomy

Prognosis depends primarily on peritoneal involvement: Confined to appendix = excellent; cellular peritoneal disease = guarded

Acellular vs. cellular peritoneal mucin is the critical prognostic distinction

CRS + HIPEC is standard treatment for peritoneal disease at specialized centers

Surveillance is risk-stratified: May not be needed for completely resected appendix-confined disease without high-risk features

Refer early to peritoneal surface malignancy specialists for suspected or confirmed peritoneal disease

Small bowel sparing in PMP is a key surgical feature allowing peritonectomy

Recurrence risk is low but real: Most recurrences occur within first 3 years

Multidisciplinary approach is essential for optimal outcomes
